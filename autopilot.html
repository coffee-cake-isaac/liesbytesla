Autopilot, Full Self Drive, and a Fair Comparison

So, this is an opinion article. It's the very first one we've done. We'll gauge how this does and determine if we'll do more in the future. There are facts in this article, so please feel free to fact check. Just want to make you aware that this may have a slightly different tone than rest of the articles. Enjoy.

Tesla's main selling point, even when compared to ICE (internal combustion engine) cars, is unequivocally the autopilot software. This, according to most Tesla owners, is one of the things that separates Tesla from any other car on the road. Autopilot's feature set is different from the Full Self Driving feature set. Allow me to break those down:

    Basic Autopilot:
  • Traffic Aware Cruise Control
  • Autosteer (Keeps you within your lane and drives with the curvature of the road you're on.
Yep... that's pretty much it for basic autopilot. This price is included in the price of the car (cars went up $2000 to include this as a feature. Note: This is a $1000 discount from the price it would have been to purchase autopilot as car DLC)

    Full Self Driving (pulled from Tesla's website):
  • Basic Summon
  • Smart Summon
  • Navigate on Autopilot
  • Autopark
  • Traffic and Stop Sign Control (Beta)
  • Auto lane change
      • Coming in a future update:
    • Auto Steer on City Streets
So, I have the Traffic and Stop Sign Control marked as beta, as that's what's listed on the support page on the Tesla website for autopilot. However, it is worth noting that Tesla considers its entire autopilot suite to be in beta. Note: This is an $8,000 option to add onto your car. Can be done at any time. The price for Full Self Driving will continue to go up as Tesla releases software updates containing features of Full Self Driving that it deems "complete".

Using the wayback machine, this wording is very different than what Tesla promised back in 2019 of April. And we'll get to this in a second.

So, I recently just went on a road trip from NC to PA. This is why there wasn't a post yesterday. It was a 9hr and 26m drive. Not a bad drive at all. Saw some beautiful parts of Virginia. Had some laughs with my wife. Got some bomb ass food at this sus diner we passed on the way up and overall had a good trip. For this trip, we decided it was better to take my wife's ICE car (2019 Volkswagen Tiguan). It has a spacious boot and a very comfortable ride. It's a lot easier for my pregnant wife to get in and out of compared to the low ride height of my Tesla Model 3. It's equipped with Traffic Aware Cruise Control and no steering/lane centering/lane keep assist. And I gotta say, the VW's TACC is far superior to Tesla's, at least in it's current state. You see, Tesla continues to improve on their software after it comes out through over-the-air updates. Something a bit unique to Tesla with other car manufacturers following behind it. Porsche and Ford have promised OTA updates. Porsche said their first update cannot be performed OTA and we've yet to see what Ford can do.

For the entire trip up, I had TACC on almost all the time. I'd say I disengaged it about the same time I'd disengage Tesla's autopilot. If we were in a city/town and I didn't know the roads, or if I was at a stop light and didn't want any jerky stop/go traffic. Just for a comparison, the Tiguan does not have any cameras what so ever to use TACC. It's all done via radar. So, imagine my surprise when (even the few times I've tried it on city streets or when someone cut me off) tight traffic didn't cause the car to slam on it's brakes. Not once, on the entire 400ish mile trip, did my wife say "what the fuck?" and I had to explain "Oh it was autopilot". This is important because it speaks volumes into how unpredictable Tesla's autopilot is. I'll be fair here and concede that when Tesla's autopilot is working and it's on a software version where everything seems to be calculated properly, it's impressive and very smooth. But this changes with every software release.

I think it's safe to say that while I did miss the autosteer part of the autopilot suite, I didn't miss the jerkiness of the TACC. It's crazy to me that on every software release, people have to say "How's autopilot this time?". But what's even crazier to me, is that Tesla has this huge suite of hardware and a very powerful set of software to interpret this hardware suite's input and yet... it still can't perform as well as a single radar on a VW Tiguan.

So, let's talk about Full Self Drive for a moment. Tesla claims this will be the future of driving. In fact, on their current autopilot page, Tesla makes bold claims such as

"When you arrive at your destination, simply step out at the entrance and your car will enter park seek mode, automatically search for a spot and park itself. A tap on your phone summons it back to you."
and

"All you will need to do is get in and tell your car where to go. If you donâ€™t say anything, your car will look at your calendar and take you there as the assumed destination. Your Tesla will figure out the optimal route, navigating urban streets, complex intersections and freeways."
However, this sounds very different than what Tesla is claiming on their support page. If you look back to the list of features, it says "autosteer on city streets". If we use the term autosteer in the current sense of autosteer for basic autopilot, that means that it's only going to keep you in your lane and follow the curves on the road. This is probably not the case, but it paints a picture of Tesla's lack of consistency and their failure to make easy-to-understand terminoloyy for consumers. So, Tesla, what'll it be? Automatic Navigation on City Streets as you mentioned in your FSD wording in April of 2019, or is it just autosteer on city streets? And if it's the former, what constituted the change in the terminology at all? Why do we as consumers have to make a guess as to what you mean, and what your car is actually capable of doing?

And why not charge $100,000 when FSD is "feature complete"? Unless, this isn't the last of the FSD tiers.
